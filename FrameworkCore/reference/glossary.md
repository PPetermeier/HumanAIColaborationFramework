# Human-AI Collaboration Framework: Glossary of Terms

This glossary organizes key concepts according to stages of skill development, from foundational understanding to advanced practice. Each section builds upon previous knowledge to support progressive learning and implementation.

## Foundation Stage: Essential Concepts

### Agency

The capacity to make intentional choices and take responsibility for directing work. In the framework, human agency means maintaining decision-making authority, evaluating outputs, and taking responsibility for outcomes despite AI assistance.

### Collaboration

An interactive partnership between human and AI that goes beyond simple tool use to create a genuine thinking relationship. True collaboration involves mutual influence, shared development, and combined capabilities.

### Context Window

The limited "memory" boundary within which an LLM can process information. Text outside this window (either not included or pushed out by other content) becomes inaccessible to the model when generating its response.

### Framework

The structured system of components, practices, and principles that supports effective human-AI collaboration. Unlike rigid methodologies, the framework provides flexible guidance that adapts to different projects and working styles.

### Next-Token Prediction

The fundamental operation of LLMs, where the model predicts the most likely next word (token) based on the sequence provided, then continues this process to generate complete responses.

### Pattern Completion

The core mechanism of how LLMs work, generating text that statistically follows the patterns in your prompt based on training data rather than retrieving answers from a knowledge database.

### Statistical Simulator

A mental model for understanding that LLMs generate text based on statistical patterns rather than true understanding, producing outputs that are plausible continuations of your input without genuine reasoning.

## Intermediate Stage: Implementation Concepts

### Anthropomorphization Management

The practice of consciously balancing the natural tendency to attribute human qualities to AI with an awareness of its non-human nature. This involves using anthropomorphization as a useful interface while maintaining clarity about the system's actual capabilities.

### Cognitive Augmentation

The strategic use of AI to extend human thinking capabilities while maintaining agency, using the complementary strengths of both human and AI to achieve results neither could accomplish alone.

### Cognitive Offloading

The process of delegating certain mental tasks to external tools or systems. Requires careful balance to enhance thinking without creating dependency or atrophying important skills.

### Devil's Advocate Session

A structured practice where ideas and arguments are systematically challenged to identify weaknesses, uncover assumptions, and strengthen reasoning.

### Intentional Prompting

The practice of deliberately crafting prompts with clear goals, consideration of the LLM's needs, awareness of assumptions, and anticipation of potential misunderstandings.

### Knowledge Bridge

Documentation that connects isolated AI interactions into a coherent progression of thought, maintaining context across the AI's episodic memory limitations.

### Metacognition

The practice of "thinking about thinking" - being aware of and developing your own thought processes. The framework actively cultivates metacognitive abilities through reflection, documentation, and strategic decision-making.

### Structured Rebellion

The deliberate, documented deviation from established patterns to explore new possibilities. Unlike random experimentation, this approach maintains clarity about why rules are being broken and what's being tested.

## Advanced Stage: System Components

### Collaborator Profile

Documentation of your working preferences, expertise, learning goals, and cognitive patterns. This serves as a blueprint for customizing collaboration to your specific needs and development objectives.

### Engagement Guidelines

Explicit documentation of how you and your AI partner will work together, including session structures, communication patterns, documentation approaches, and quality standards.

### Living Questions

A dynamic document tracking your evolving understanding, open inquiries, and developing insights. Unlike static documentation, this captures the growth of knowledge rather than just its current state.

### Project Foundations

Documentation of core project parameters including purpose, scope, key definitions, assumptions, theoretical approaches, and quality standards.

### Project Plan

Systematic organization of work components, timelines, dependencies, resource allocations, and progress tracking mechanisms.

### Session Template

Structure for individual working sessions that maintains continuity, documents progress, and supports effective knowledge transfer between interactions.

## Expert Stage: Advanced Practices

### Active Evaluation

The disciplined practice of critically examining LLM outputs for accuracy, bias, completeness, and relevance rather than accepting them at face value.

### Automation Bias

The tendency to trust computer-generated information due to its authoritative presentation. Requires developing habits of verification and maintaining healthy skepticism, especially for factual claims.

### Confirmation Bias Management

Practices for counteracting the natural tendency to accept information that confirms existing beliefs, including deliberately seeking disconfirming perspectives and multiple viewpoints.

### Feedback Loops

Systematic practices for improving collaboration over time, including documenting successful prompting patterns, noting particularly helpful or problematic outputs, and experimenting with different approaches.

### Iterative Refinement

The approach of viewing initial LLM responses as drafts rather than final products, providing specific feedback, refining prompts, and guiding the LLM toward increasingly useful outputs.

### Meta-Collaboration

The practice of actively collaborating about the collaboration itself - examining, discussing, and improving how you work together rather than just what you're working on.

### Strategic Task Selection

The metacognitive skill of identifying which tasks benefit most from LLM assistance versus those requiring human judgment, including developing hybrid approaches that combine strengths of both.

## Mastery Stage: Integration Concepts

### Augmented Cognition

A mental model viewing LLMs as extensions of human thinking capability, creating cognitive possibilities that neither human nor LLM could achieve independently. Focuses on how LLMs enhance human capabilities when used skillfully.

### Collaborative Partner

A mental model viewing LLMs as thinking partners that help explore ideas, consider alternatives, and refine thinking, despite having no genuine understanding or agency. This perspective maximizes value through genuine back-and-forth exchanges.

### Critical Evaluation

The ability to assess information, arguments, and outputs with rigorous, nuanced judgment rather than simple acceptance or rejection.

### Evolution Tracking

Systematic documentation of how understanding, approaches, and outputs change over time, capturing not just what is known but how knowledge develops.

### Integration

The practice of maintaining coherence across different project components, ensuring that developments in one area appropriately influence others.

### Knowledge Development

The systematic growth of understanding that goes beyond information gathering to include refinement of mental models, recognition of patterns, and development of insights.

### Mirror

A mental model recognizing that LLMs often reflect back your own thinking patterns, clarity, and assumptions. When LLM outputs seem confused, it may indicate lack of clarity in your own thinking or communication.

### Self-Understanding

Deeper awareness of your own cognitive patterns, preferences, strengths, and growth areas developed through structured collaboration.

### Strategic Thinking

The capacity to make decisions with consideration of broader context, long-term implications, systemic relationships, and underlying patterns.

### Tool-with-Feedback-Loop

A mental model emphasizing that LLMs become more effective through skillful guidance and output refinement. Working with LLMs is inherently iterative rather than a one-and-done process.

## Ethical and Psychological Dimensions

### Agency-Augmentation Balance

The ongoing process of maintaining human decision-making authority and judgment while strategically leveraging AI capabilities to enhance cognitive processes.

### Ethical Responsibility

Recognition that the human collaborator bears responsibility for the ethical implications of work produced with AI assistance, including verifying factual accuracy, preventing harm, and ensuring beneficial outcomes.

### Power Dynamics

The recognition of the inherent power relationship in human-AI collaboration, where humans have authority over and responsibility for the interaction while acknowledging the influential capabilities of AI systems.

### Reflective Expertise

The advanced state of metacognitive development characterized by continuous refinement of collaborative approaches based on experience, deliberate practice, and systematic reflection.

### Trust Calibration

The ongoing process of developing appropriate levels of trust in AI systems based on verified performance rather than unwarranted confidence or skepticism, adjusting collaboration approaches accordingly.
